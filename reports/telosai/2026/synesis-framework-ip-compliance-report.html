🔄 REVISED FINDING — Original "CRITICAL: Must Rename" downgraded after legal analysis

The initial assessment classified "Myers-Briggs" as a hard rename-or-die situation. That analysis assumed Synesis was administering its own test and labeling it Myers-Briggs — which would be trademark infringement. The actual Synesis model is fundamentally different: users import results from genuine MBTI® instruments administered by licensed third parties, and Synesis post-processes those results through its synthesis engine. This is a nominative fair use posture — you can use a trademark to refer to the trademarked product itself, provided you follow specific legal safeguards.

Nominative Fair Use — Legal Framework

The Three-Part Test (New Kids on the Block, adopted by 9th Circuit; applied in Earth, Wind & Fire 2024)

  1. Necessity: The product must not be readily identifiable without using the trademark.
    ✅ SATISFIED — There is no generic alternative name for the MBTI® instrument. "Cognitive Type" or "16 Typologies" would not communicate to a user who already took the MBTI that they can import those specific results.
  2. Limited use: Only as much of the mark as is used as is reasonably necessary.
    ✅ ACHIEVABLE — Use "Myers-Briggs" or "MBTI" only on the score import screen and in the user's personal results display. Do NOT use the trademark in primary marketing headlines, hero CTAs, or as a product category label.
  3. No endorsement implied: The user does nothing suggesting sponsorship or endorsement by the trademark holder.
    ⚠️ REQUIRES SAFEGUARDS — Per Earth, Wind & Fire 2024 ruling, a clear disclaimer is mandatory. The court rejected a defense where only the word "alumni" was used. Synesis MUST include explicit disclaimer language on every screen where the trademark appears.

Source: Earth, Wind & Fire IP, LLC v. Substantial Music Grp. (S.D. Fla. 2024) — nominative fair use defense failed because no clear disclaimer was present. "Merely using the word 'alumni' was insufficient to dispel consumer confusion." The court affirmed that the three-part test is the governing standard.

Required Safeguards for "Myers-Briggs" Usage

1. Mandatory Disclaimer — Every Screen With the Mark

Required Disclaimer Language

Myers-Briggs Type Indicator®, Myers-Briggs®, and MBTI® are registered trademarks of
Myers & Briggs Foundation in the United States and other countries. Synesis is not
affiliated with, endorsed by, or sponsored by Myers & Briggs Foundation or
The Myers-Briggs Company. Users import their own previously-administered results.
Synesis does not administer the MBTI® instrument.

2. ® Symbol Usage

® at most prominent use and first use on every screen where the mark appears. Use the full registered name once per screen: "Myers-Briggs Type Indicator®" then shorthand "MBTI®" thereafter.

3. Adjective-Only Rule

Always: "MBTI® instrument," "MBTI® results," "Myers-Briggs® assessment." Never: "Take an MBTI," "Your MBTI is INTJ." The mark must modify a noun — it describes the source of the user's imported data, not Synesis's product.

4. Import-Only Framing

Every CTA and label must communicate import, not administration: "Import your MBTI® type" · "I already know my Myers-Briggs® results" · "Enter your 4-letter type from a previously administered MBTI® instrument." Never: "Take the MBTI assessment" · "Get your MBTI type" · "MBTI test."

5. No Instrument Reproduction

Synesis must never reproduce, display, or simulate the actual MBTI® questions, items, or scoring algorithm. The import flow accepts only the final 4-letter type code — not individual question responses. This is a bright line: we process results, we do not administer the test.
