The Moxley Town Chronicle

October 1882 – April 1883. A single street of sun-baked false-front buildings on a bend of a river that dries up half the year. The railroad is coming. A Chicago speculator is buying everything. The townsite sits on contested land — Spanish grant overlay on Comanche hunting grounds overlay on Anglo squatters' claims.

Each character's private drama accumulates into a collective portrait of a community at the moment of its transformation. Structural model: Winesburg, Ohio filtered through frontier violence. Highest originality.

The Bitterroot Trail

April – October 1874. A freighting expedition — twelve wagons, sixty mules, thirty-seven people — carries secret mining equipment from San Antonio to Montana Territory. The cargo is destined for treaty-protected Lakota land.

Five POV characters, four parts, ninety-six chapters. The expedition is the crucible inside which every character is tested. Proven journey structure with novel moral stakes.

"The bar is the Pulitzer. Do not aim lower." — The Evaluator Perspective

The Ensemble

Fifteen characters, each a departure from or deepening of a Lonesome Dove archetype — with deliberate emphasis on perspectives the novel could not or did not include

Eulalia Quince

The Widow Who Outlasted the Country · age 58

Married at 15 to a whiskey-runner killed by Regulators. Held his Brazos claim alone for forty years — buried three hired hands, shot two horse thieves, raised a son who left for California and never wrote. Clara Allen without family or purpose — pure stubborn endurance against the accumulating silence.

High Novelty

Moses "Mose" Whitfield

Buffalo Soldier Turned Tracker · age 42

Born enslaved, emancipated at 16, enlisted in the 10th Cavalry at 18. Served fourteen years on the southern plains. Mustered out to find no place for a Black man with a cavalry pension and a vocabulary that makes white settlers uneasy. A Deets who knows the cage — literate, bitter, self-aware.

High Novelty

Dempsey Crouch

The Gospel-Nerve Drifter · age 34

Circuit-rider's son who can quote scripture end to end and has killed six men — he counts them on the same fingers that turned the pages of his mother's Bible. Drifts from town to town, preaching funerals for strangers. Believes in God and believes he is damned — not for the killing, but because he enjoyed the first one.

Medium Novelty

Linh Tam

The Railroad Widow with a Repeating Rifle · age 27

Came from Guangdong in 1867. Her husband was killed in a Sierra tunnel blast — his body never recovered. She spent three years in railroad camps, learned to shoot and ride astride. Now crossing a thousand miles alone in the year of the Chinese Exclusion Act, searching for her husband's murdered cousin. Carries a Henry rifle. Listens more than she speaks.

High Novelty

Ismael "Izzy" Pacheco

The Tejano Who Rode with Quantrill · age 39

Lost his family's Rio Grande land to gringo courts. At 16, full of rage, fell in with Quantrill's raiders — rode through Lawrence in 1863. Twenty years trying to outrun what he saw. Works as a horse breaker — the only trade that asks nothing of his tongue. Gentles horses by breathing into their nostrils. The only thing that makes him weep is music.

High Novelty

Wanbli Tokahe (Eagle First)

The Comanche Who Learned White Laws · age 33

Born Nokoni Comanche on the Llano Estacado. Captured at 12, sent to Carlisle Indian School, renamed "Thomas Logan." Returns west as government interpreter — trusted by neither whites nor his own people. Speaks four languages and is fluent in despair. What Lonesome Dove could not see: the educated native caught between worlds.

High Novelty

Hattie Peak

The Schoolteacher Inventing Her Own Rules · age 31

Daughter of a failed Forty-Niner. Taught herself to read from Ivanhoe. Has taught school in six territories, always leaving when expectations pressed too close. A wealthy rancher has proposed marriage. If she says yes, she gains safety and loses herself. Clara Allen who never married — the tragedy is she may never find an anchor, or may find one too late.

Medium Novelty

Floyd "Sog" Boggs

The Innocent Who Should Not Survive · age 19

Foundling raised by a Fort Smith whore who died of consumption when he was fourteen. Survived by doing whatever people told him — digging graves, skinning carcasses, once accidentally helping rob a stagecoach. Has never met a horse he could not gentle. Newt without Call or Gus to protect him — the innocence that gets preyed upon, not sheltered.

High Novelty

Lydia "Liddy" Strange

The Whore with a Business Plan · age 44

Bought her freedom at 26. Owns the only saloon in Moxley plus half the lots on main street. Unofficial mayor, banker, and confessor. The railroad is coming and a Chicago speculator wants everything. Calls everyone "honey" — it is never affectionate. Gus McCrae would have adored her; Call would have disapproved, which would have amused her enormously.

Medium Novelty

Thaddeus Grimes

The Blacksmith Who Believes in Civilization · age 48

Born free in Boston. Founded Moxley's first smithy, became the town's moral center over twenty years — judges disputes, holds debts, reads the Bible in Greek. A younger Black man has been lynched in the next county. His wife is begging him to leave for Kansas. His faith in progress and law is being tested against the growing machinery of racial violence.

High Novelty

Poor Tom Allbee

The Failed Hatter Who Won't Quit · age 52

Apprenticed to a Philadelphia hatter at 11. Struck west at 25 with beaver pelts and a dream. The beaver trade collapsed. Twenty-five years of failed enterprises followed — a mercantile that burned, a freight line that went bankrupt, a gold claim that produced seventeen dollars. Now runs a hat shop in a town where no one wears hats. Has one last scheme involving an ostrich farm.

Medium Novelty

Kizzi Bowdre

The Girl Who Got to Keep Her Gun · age 16

Daughter of a Comanchero trader and a Mexican captive. Her father taught her to shoot, track, skin, and trust no one. When he died of fever, she buried him alone and took his horse, rifle, and mule. Summoned to Moxley by a lawyer claiming her father owned land. The wilderness made her free; the law would cage her. Feral in a way Lorena Wood was never allowed to be.

High Novelty

Captain Josiah Ledbetter

The Withered Cavalryman · age 63

Rose from enlisted man to brevet colonel — Shiloh, Stones River, Chickamauga. Spent twenty years on the frontier chasing raiders and signing treaties he knew would be broken. Wife left in 1872. Children are strangers. His last command: a remote outpost with thirty men, a corrupt supply officer, and a reservation where the government hasn't delivered rations in three months. Call after the outfit dispersed, after the last friend was buried.

Medium Novelty

Patrick "P.O." Dunnigan

The Railroad Surveyor Who Saw Too Much · age 45

Born in County Cork, fled the Famine at 12. Union Army sapper turned surveyor for the Union Pacific. Mapped more of the West than any man alive and never owned a single acre. His maps are works of art; his life is a shambles. The advance man for the railroad that will destroy the world his companions inhabit. Every stake he drives is a nail in the coffin of the old West — and he knows it.

High Novelty

Senora Antonia Covarrubias

The Hacendada Without an Hacienda · age 66

Born to one of the great land-grant families of New Mexico. Watched 300,000 acres dissolve through American courts, bad marriages, and taxes her father never understood. Lives in a four-room adobe with nine cats, writing a family history no one will publish. Remembers the West before the Americans came — and is not sentimental about it.

The Land as Character

Eight locations — each a narrative engine, not a backdrop

Moxley, Texas

Town · Gravitational Center

A single street of sun-baked false-front buildings crouched on a river bend. Saloon, smithy, mercantile, schoolhouse with one window, more dogs than children. Built by people who expected more of the future than the future delivered.

The Staked Plains

Wilderness · Purgatorio

Table-flat expanse of shortgrass and sky extending to every horizon without a wrinkle. Travelers have gone mad circling their own tracks. Where the old ways still hold — Comanche trails, buffalo skulls, bones of settlers who didn't find water.

Fort Belknap (Abandoned)

Fort · Site of Memory

Crumbling limestone barracks, parade ground overgrown with mesquite. Flagpole stands but no flag flies. A single old soldier paid to be caretaker of a place everyone else has forgotten.

Covarrubias Grant Cemetery

Ranch · Last Stand of the Old World

Adobe enclosure with crumbling church, century-old pecan tree, eighty-seven graves — Spanish, Mexican, Comanche, Anglo — rotting in the same red dirt. Not all the graves are marked with names, and not all the names are the ones on the stones.

The Crossing at Palo Duro

Crossing · Threshold

Shallow ford over the Red River where cottonwoods grow thick. A worn sign warns of Comanche country, riddled with bullet holes. A half-sunk ferry on the north bank — monument to someone's abandoned dream.

Pacheco Horse Camp

Wilderness · Hermitage

Hidden canyon in the Canadian River breaks. Brush arbor, rope corral, stone hearth. Smell of horse and woodsmoke is permanent. Izzy Pacheco lives here alone for months, emerging only when the loneliness becomes unbearable.

Allbee's Fine Hats and Sundries

Town · Comic Relief That Curdles

Dusty storefront crammed with unsold hats, expired patent medicines, a taxidermied jackrabbit in a waistcoat. Inventory unchanged in three years. The gap between aspiration and reality made physical.

Strange's Saloon & Social

Outpost · The Town's Heart

Only saloon in Moxley, also the bank, post office, and courthouse. Mahogany bar shipped from St. Louis at ruinous expense. The mirror behind it is the only one between Dallas and Santa Fe. Neutral ground where every station meets.

Part I — The Gathering

14 chapters~48,000 wordsApril 1874

San Antonio as pressure-cooker. Each character arrives carrying a secret, a wound, or a desperate hope. The expedition assembles through intersecting decisions. Climax: the rain-swollen Guadalupe River crossing — the first test, Caleb freezes mid-river, Emmett drags him through. "The river was behind them. The country opened ahead. And Emmett Cole understood, for the first time, that he might not come back."

POV Introduction Order: Emmett Cole (1) → Two Bears (2) → Ruth Tolliver (3) → Emmett (4) → Isaiah Brown (5) → Caleb Cross (6) → Ruth (7) → Emmett (8–9) → Caleb (10) → Two Bears (11) → Ensemble/Emmett (12) → Emmett (13) → Ensemble climax (14)

Part II — The Crossing

30 chapters~114,000 wordsMay–June 1874

The Llano Estacado, Indian Territory, the long haul toward the Arkansas. Endurance, slow accumulation of pressure, revelation of character under deprivation. Water shortages, a Comanche scare, a desertion, the first death. The expedition begins to become a community — then begins to fracture. Climax: reaching the Arkansas River at a cost. A teamster dies of snakebite. Caleb kills for the second time. Isaiah receives word his daughter has died of fever before he could free her.

Part III — The High Plains

28 chapters~100,000 wordsJuly–August 1874

The escalation. Lakota territory. The cargo's true nature revealed — mining equipment for illegal extraction on treaty land. Two Bears' band attacks. Isaiah is killed covering the wagons. Caleb murders a teamster in cold blood. Ruth's past catches up. The expedition that left San Antonio no longer exists. Emmett confronts Vane and learns the full conspiracy.

Part IV — The Reckoning

24 chapters~88,000 wordsSeptember–October 1874

The end of the trail. Three-part climax: (1) Caleb killed — by Emmett after a final violent act, or by Lakota warriors; (2) Two Bears dies in a last attack on the mining site, ensuring the operation cannot proceed without Army protection; (3) Emmett confronts Vane and makes the choice that defines him. The machinery is destroyed. The survivors scatter. Last image: Emmett on a horse, riding south, alone.

Six Binding Creative Decisions

The Evaluator's verdict — what must hold for the narrative to succeed

1. The 1880s — The Elegy Window

NON-REVERSIBLE · Source: Evaluator

Set in the 1880s (1882–1883 for town model, 1874 for journey model) — the open range's last decade. Barbed wire going up, Indian Wars concluding, buffalo near extinction. Characters know they're late. The elegy requires historical specificity.

2. Working Protagonist, Not Gunslinger

REVERSIBLE · Source: Evaluator

Rancher, drover, horse trader, or freighter — not a lawman or gunfighter. The story is about making a living, not making a name. Labor gives texture, stakes, and a built-in clock. A rancher's story is about life, which makes death matter more.

3. Co-Equal Woman's POV

NON-REVERSIBLE · Source: Evaluator

At least one woman with her own arc, stakes, and climax — not a subplot but a parallel narrative thread. The single greatest weakness of the Western genre is the marginalization of women. Historically honest and narratively strengthening.

4. Antagonist with Legitimate Grievance

REVERSIBLE · Source: Evaluator

The villain must have a worldview the reader can't entirely dismiss even while fearing them. A villain who is just bad is dramatically inert. The best conflict is ideological, not merely violent.

5. Pre-Final-Act Primary Death

NON-REVERSIBLE · Source: Evaluator

Kill at least one primary character before the final act. The death should feel wasteful, random, and unbearably sad. Establishes that the world doesn't care about narrative justice. The randomness IS the theme.

6. Dual Male Protagonist Engine

REVERSIBLE · Source: Evaluator

Two co-equal male protagonists with opposing philosophies bound by shared history — the Call/Gus dynamic. Love that cannot be spoken, expressed through action, protection, sacrifice. Generates plot, theme, and emotional weight more efficiently than any other structural choice.

Thematic Territory

Six thematic clusters, each traced from introduction through development, subversion, to final payoff

The Cost of Belonging

Every character faces a variant of the same question: what are you willing to surrender in exchange for community? Hattie would surrender her independence; Mose would surrender his invisibility; Wanbli would surrender his identity; Liddy would surrender her autonomy. The town of Moxley itself — sell to the railroad or cling to a dying way of life.

The Violence at the Foundation

Moxley is built on stolen land, and everyone is implicated. The Spanish took it from the Comanche, the Anglos took it from the Spanish, and the law that governs it was written by the people who did the final taking. Characters carry this knowledge in different ways — Izzy (participated in worse violence), Wanbli (his people were the victims), Thaddeus (his people were enslaved by the legal system's authors).

The Inadequacy of the Law

The legal system appears not as justice but as a commodity — bought by the speculator, wielded against the weak, incomprehensible to the uneducated. Characters who ignore the law (Liddy, Izzy) or learn to manipulate it (Wanbli, Thaddeus) fare best. Those who trust it — Poor Tom, Kizzi, Eulalia — are its victims.

The Elegy for a World That Never Was

LD mourns the end of the cattle-drive West, but that West was built on the destruction of earlier worlds. Senora Covarrubias mourns the Spanish West; Wanbli Tokahe mourns the Comanche West; Eulalia Quince mourns the settler West. The deepest sadness: everyone is mourning a loss, and everyone's loss was someone else's gain.

The Body as Archive

The West lives in the body — in scars, calluses, the way a man sits a horse or a woman carries water. Linh's hands remember washing clothes in icy Sierra streams. Mose's back remembers the whip. As the world changes, these embodied knowledges become obsolete — useless skills for a future that has no use for them.

What Men Do to Each Other in the Name of Love

The great unspoken of the Western. Love between men — between soldiers, partners, strangers who recognize each other across a fire — that cannot be named and must be expressed in action: protection, sacrifice, violence. Dempsey's attachment to his dead friend. Izzy's responsibility for Sog. Captain Ledbetter's devotion to a command that has never loved him back.

The Bridge Reader Is Everything

The reader who wants BOTH — momentum AND depth, violence AND meaning, landscape AND interiority, tradition AND reckoning — is the novel's actual target audience. Traditional Western readers alone cannot sustain a 600-page literary novel commercially. Literary readers alone won't pick up a Western without bridge-reader evangelism. Lose them in the middle act and you lose everything. Win them and they become the book's most powerful ambassadors — because they occupy the same intersection the novel does.

Adaptation & Publishing

Screen Potential: HIGH

Ideal format: 6–10 episode prestige limited series (HBO, FX, Amazon, Apple). The ensemble density requires room to breathe — a 2-hour feature can't support the character weight. Casting is everything (see: Duvall as Gus).

Estimated Draft: 18 Months

Three-draft approach: (1) Part I complete + skeletal Parts II–IV (6 months), (2) full draft at 250K words (6 months), (3) expansion to 350K with full set pieces (6 months). Each stage produces a complete, readable artifact.

Execution Specifications

The four documents that must exist before Chapter 1 is written

1. Character Dossiers

Pre-Draft Deliverable

Full physical description, voice/dialect sample, backstory before chapter 1, core want vs. core need (Dramatica-style), fear inventory, skill set, death note for doomed characters. Each dossier 2–3 pages. Non-negotiable: every POV character's voice must be distinguishable on an unmarked page of dialogue.

2. Geography & Routes Map

Pre-Draft Deliverable

Full map of Moxley and surrounding territory OR the 1,800-mile San Antonio–Montana route with all waypoints, water sources, and dangers. Llano Estacado geography verified against historical sources. Seasonal weather pattern overlay. Must be complete before any travel/location chapters are drafted.

3. Timeline & Event Log

Pre-Draft Deliverable

Day-by-day timeline for the narrative's timespan. Character location at every chapter. Historical events happening concurrently. Distance traveled per day. Master spreadsheet tracking: who is in every scene, what each character knows at each point, what the reader knows vs. what each character knows.

4. Dialect & Dialogue Guide

Pre-Draft Deliverable

Period-appropriate vocabulary by region (Texas, Lakota territory, New Mexico). Education markers by character. Profanity policy (period-appropriate, not modern). Racial address conventions of the period. One non-negotiable rule: no anachronistic language. Verify every word that feels modern.
